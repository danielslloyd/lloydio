Stalin prioritized Soviet state interests over international communist solidarity, directing the Chinese Communist Party (CCP) to subordinate itself to Chiang Kai-shek's Nationalist Kuomintang (KMT) in a United Front strategy during the 1920s. This proved catastrophic when Chiang turned on his communist allies in the 1927 Shanghai Massacre, slaughtering thousands of CCP members who had been ordered not to arm themselves or resist. Stalin later reversed course but continued to hedge his bets, consistently treating the CCP as a bargaining chip in Soviet geopolitical dealings rather than a revolutionary movement worth protecting.

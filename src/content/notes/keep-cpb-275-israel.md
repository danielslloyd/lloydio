In the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Jewish immigration into Palestine was opposed by the Arab inhabitants, who were mostly not landowners. The Ottoman rulers, though ostensibly sympathetic to their co-religionists, could be bribed and mostly looked the other way. When the British took over the Palestine Mandate, they encouraged Jewish immigration (under the Balfour Declaration), resulting in a sharp increase in the Jewish population. Tensions quickly escalated as Arab nationalism emerged, and increasingly violent Arab revolts and riots broke out in the 1920s and 1930s

In 1937, the Peel Commission recommended a 2-state solution, with a Jewish state containing Galilee and much of the Mediterranean coast, and with Britain retaining a strip of land encompassing Jerusalem and Bethlehem and extending to Jaffa. The commission proposed resettling hundreds of thousands of Arabs from the proposed Jewish state and a few hundred Jews from the proposed Arab state

In 1939, after stamping out a major Arab revolt and facing the prospect of war in Europe, Britain reversed course and issued a white paper stating its intentions for drastically reduced Jewish immigration and Palestinian statehood within 10 years. The aftermath of the crushed revolt weakened Arab military capability, while Jewish volunteers serving with the British army gained valuable experience

In 1946, against the backdrop of terrorist activities by hardline Jewish paramilitary groups (e.g., the Irgun / IZL and Lehi / Stern Gang), the 12-person "Anglo-American Committee" recommended placing Palestine under UN trusteeship (with an eventual goal of either a 1- or 2-state solution) and increasing Jewish immigration. This recommendation was vehemently rejected by the Arab community, leading Britain to ask the UN for help in April 1947

The UN set up a special committee on Palestine (UNSCOP) that toured the region, met with Zionist representatives (Arabs boycotted) and recommended a partition plan. This resolution was supported by the Soviet Union, actively lobbied for by the USA, and quietly opposed by the UK. It passed by a narrow vote in the UN General Assembly on November 27, 1947.

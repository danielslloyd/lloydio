Before Pope Gregory VII's ”Gregorian Reforms" Catholic priests could buy clerical appointments (known as "simony") and many had wives and/or concubines ("nicolaitism"). Clerical appointments could also be bequeathed to sons, which led to corruption of the church. Gregory VII was also a key player in the "Investiture Controversy" over the ability to choose and install clergy. It began as a power struggle between Gregory VII and Henry IV (then King, later Holy Roman Emperor) and ended nearly 50 years later when Pope Callixtus II and Emperor Henry V agreed on the Concordat of Worms, which required bishops to swear an oath of fealty to the secular monarch but left selection to the church.

Galen treated Christianity as a philosophy rather than a superstition because of the way it led Christians to live lives of virtue, and was the first pagan author to do so. He criticized Christianity for expecting adherents to take teachings on faith, rather than thinking through syllogisms. He also (like most philosophers of his day) took issue with the idea of a god pre-existing the natural world and not himself bound by laws. This doctrine of creation ex nihilo became central to Christian teaching as an differentiator from pagan philosophy.

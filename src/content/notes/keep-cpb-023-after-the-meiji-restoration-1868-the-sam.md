After the Meiji Restoration (1868), the samurai class was quickly dismantled. Samurai lost their stipends, status symbols, and social roles virtually overnight, and they and their sons flooded missionary schools seeking Westernized educations. Because missionaries were initially confined to cities and barred from preaching, the first Japanese Christians were overwhelmingly students at these language schools, and almost all were jobless samurai, who often found that Christianity's emphasis on personal ethics and self-sacrifice mapped naturally onto bushido values. This cemented a lasting association between Japanese Christianity and a highly educated elite: in a country that is less than 1% Christian, 7 of Japan's 35 post-WWII Prime Ministers have been Christian.

Before the Seven Years' War, Britain wanted to protect the Netherlands (strategic ally) and Hanover (George II's homeland). Austria was more focused on Prussia than France, so Britain had trouble getting them to commit resources to Dutch defense, and British public opinion was far more worried about Dutch defense than Hanoverian.

At the outset of the Seven Years' War, Britain sent three expeditions: Braddock, a naval expedition to intercept French reinforcements, and a New England militia expedition to Nova Scotia. The naval expedition was partially successful and the militia took Nova Scotia, but Braddock was utterly defeated. The upshot was that Britain appeared to be the aggressor in the Seven Years War but had not achieved a decisive victory. Thus France was not discouraged from entering the war.

At the outset of the Seven Years' War, France and Austria signed a treaty of mutual defense whereby France also agreed not to invade the Netherlands, which was very strategically limiting in a war with Britain

Leading up to the Seven Years' War, the French were responding to English trading (not settlement) in the Ohio river valley. Superior shipping and industry allowed English traders to offer better deals to native fur traders.

When the French and English were setting the new world, the French would claim entire river valleys /drainage basins at a time (e.g., the Ohio River valley), while the English preferred drawing lines on the map

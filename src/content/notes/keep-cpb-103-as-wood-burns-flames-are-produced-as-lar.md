As wood burns, flames are produced as large polymers break down into smaller molecules - these evaporate around 600°F and glow before combusting. When these small molecules are gone, only the carbon structure (charcoal) remains, which does not evaporate and merely glows as it combusts around 900°F. Charcoal can be manufactured by limiting a wood fire's oxygen supply to keep it between 600-900°F.

Traditional vaccines work by introducing the actual virus, or part of it, into the body to induce an immune response. To avoid actually causing disease, the virus is weakened or inactivated, or only a piece (subunit) of the virus is used. Manufacturing these vaccines requires growing the virus in large quantities (in chicken eggs for flu vaccines, for example), which takes months.
Messenger RNA (mRNA) vaccines don’t use an actual virus – just instructions for cells to assemble proteins – and these can be synthesized in about a week. For the COVID-19 vaccine, the mRNA used contains assembly instructions for the “spike" protein that the virus uses to bind to and invade cells. Those antibodies should then work to fend off infection if the patient is exposed to the actual virus. Since only a piece the virus is replicated, mRNA vaccines eliminate the risk of a “bad batch” actually causing disease, as occurred during the early rollout of polio vaccine.
-Akino Yamashita, 11/19, The Dispatch
